Significance

Significance (α) is the probability of finding a false positive, which is also called a Type I Error.

Significance is one of two important error probabilities that must balanced when designing an experiment.


Generally speaking, scientific hypotheses concern a causal relationship between a treatment or manipulation and an outcome. Experiments are conducted to build evidence for (or against) a scientific hypothesis. Since the burden of proof for the scientific conjecture is on the scientist, we phrase this process statistically in terms of the Null Hypothesis that the treatment has no effect, sort of like the legal principle of “innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.”

The Significance, or Type I error probability, is our quantification of “reasonable doubt.” Type II error, on the other hand, represents the chances of letting a guilty party go free in the legal analogy. Power, the complement of Type II error, is the likelihood of detecting guilt (that is, a finding a true positive).

  • If Significance is too small, we are more likely to miss a true positive
  • If Significance is too large, we are more likely to find a false positive

Such results may:

  1. inappropriately reduce interest in the treatment that really works, or
  2. encourage further studies in an ineffective treatment, wasting time, money, resources, and subjects.
