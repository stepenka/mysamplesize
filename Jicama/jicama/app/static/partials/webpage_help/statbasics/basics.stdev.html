Standard Deviation

Standard deviation measures the spread, variation, or dispersion among outcome variable values across a population or sample.

Knowledge of the Standard Deviation of your outcome measure is crucial to sample size determination and experimental design.

  • Detecting a true positive result requires enough samples to average out the variation in test subjects, variation that is measured by the standard deviation.
  • With a small standard deviation, fewer subjects are needed.
  • A large standard deviation may require many subjects.

It can be helpful in early stages of research to reduce variability through careful control of the study population. The biological similarity of inbred, cloned subjects can help keep standard deviations low and improve the signal to noise ratio of your experiment. Moving a potential therapeutic agent from investigation of efficacy in a controlled setting to a more robust understanding for human populations with many dimensions of variation takes careful scoping and sequencing of studies, starting with homogeneous populations and slowly increasing variability in subsequent experiments.

Pilot studies are crucial for obtaining an estimate of the standard deviation. Unfortunately, the same population variation that brings uncertainty into analysis of confirmatory experiments also creates uncertainty in pilot study estimates. And, of course, one must design the pilot study itself. Nonetheless, we may wish to account for uncertainty in the standard deviation estimated from a pilot study. One approach is to use an inflation factor derived from confidence interval upper bounds for the standard deviation. One difficult issue arises when the standard deviation changes as a function of a treatment or some other experimental factor. You may be familiar with Levene’s test for homogeneity of variance, which helps one detect this situation in experimental data. We note that MySampleSize’s tools assume homogeneous variance.

Our power and sample size calculations rely on some very specific (but commonly used) assumptions about variability: the outcome measure is normally distributed, and the standard deviation does not depend on treatment settings or factor levels. If you know that your experimental system violates either of these assumptions, please contact us for consultation.
