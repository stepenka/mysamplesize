Case Study 1: Simple Randomized Design
Effects of metabotropic glutamate receptor agonist, DCG-IV, on acute neuronal degeneration after traumatic brain injury in rats.

objective

The role of metabotropic glutamate receptor activation after traumatic brain injury (TBI) is not well understood. In vitro studies suggest that activation of Group II metabotropic glutamate receptors may provide some degree of neuroprotection and may be potential targets for the development of therapeutic strategies.

  • This examined the effects of a Group II selective agonist on neuronal degeneration after in vivo TBI.

hypothesis

Pharmacological activation of Group II metabotropic glutamate receptors with compound DCG-IV will reduce neuronal cell degeneration measured at 24 hours after experimental TBI in rats.

methods

Rats were randomly assigned to the following groups;

Vehicle control		 20 fmol DCG-IV 100 fmol DCG-IV 500 fmol DCG-IV

Anesthetized rats were administered a moderate magnitude of fluid percussion TBI. Five minutes after TBI, DCG-IV or aCSF was slowly infused into the lateral ventricle over a 1 hour. At 24 hours after TBI, rats were euthanized and brains processed for fluoro-jade histology to detect degenerating neurons.

Degenerating cells were counted in 4 coronal sections in the hippocampus were in a 1.5 mm block of brain tissue.

design considerations

We are conducting a simple exploratory study to investigate pharmacological activation of Group II metabotropic glutamate receptors. To keep the study as simple as possible, we are only examining a single treatment type with a small number of treatment settings.

Outcome Measure

One response or outcome measure is taken, namely the count of fluoro-jade positive neurons.

Treatment

One treatment is applied (DCG-IV) at three different settings. The settings were chosen from experience to be able to stimulate responses of scientific significance. An additional experimental group is the vehicle control to ensure that the treatment delivery vehicle is not the source of the treatment response.

Factors and covariates

No properties of the experimental units (individual subjects) were deemed important sources of variation for which we need to control.

These considerations lead to a simple randomized design and a one way ANOVA statistical analysis with four groups (one control and three treatment groups). To plan the study, we need to balance the number of subjects per group against our ability to detect a difference among the mean responses.

To plan the study, we examine the statistical power, detectable signal-to-noise ratio, and sample size. The null hypothesis is that all three treatments will have the same mean level of response as does the control. The alternative hypothesis is that at least one of these four mean responses will be different. Type I error is rejecting the null hypothesis when it is true (a false positive), and we choose a significance level of ?=0.05 as the likelihood of a type I error. Type II error is failing to reject the null hypothesis when the alternative is true. Rejecting the null when the alternative is true is detecting a true positive. The likelihood detecting a true positive, the statistical power of the test, is determined by our specification of alpha=0.05, but it also depends on the sample size, the actual difference in means, and the standard deviation of the outcome measure. The detectable relative effect size or detectable signal-to-noise ratio is the ratio of the actual mean difference to the standard deviation.

In the following plots we examine the sample size required to achieve certain selected levels of statistical power, as a function of the detectable relative effect size.
