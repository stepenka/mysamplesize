Categorical Factors

Nontreatment factors are properties of the subjects that may introduce additional variation in the outcome measure. To minimize the number of subjects required, experimentalists group similar subjects together by their nontreatment factor levels, rather than randomly assigning subjects to treatment settings regardless of their nontreatment factor levels. One could, for example, accidentally assign all male subjects to the treatment and all females to the control, thus confounding any positive outcome, as the result might be due to variations arising from sex instead of the treatment. This process of "blocking" helps disambiguate positive outcomes from nontreatment based variations.

Categorical factors are discrete variables. For example, subject weight can be a (categorical) factor -- low, medium, and high as levels. Some factors might be:

  • Blood Type
  • Weight (e.g. low, medium, high)
