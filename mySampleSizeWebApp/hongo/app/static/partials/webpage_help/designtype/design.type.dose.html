Dose Reponse

Dose Response designs are used to investigate the effect of a continuous increasing level of a variable (usually Dose) on a measured response.

This design is similar to a comparative design, or a Factorial design, with a continuous variable. However it is defined as having just one factor as a treatment (dose level) whereas a factorial design can have 2 or more factors.

The use of a continuous variable may more closely reflect the biology of your experiment making it a more appropriate design for your experimental goals. For example, if you are more interested in if the results show an statistically significant effect of a dose at level 3 compared to level 4 and 5 instead of if the effect of level 3 is statistically significantly different from the control. This allows a researcher to better assess the dose-response relationship instead of the effect of each dose level compared to the control.

Design Considerations

  • Dose Levels should be equally spaced on a log scale
  • Number of doses tested should be one more than the number of parameters to be estimated.

Defining the Logistic Curve (the Hill equation)

The most common model in dose response models is a four parameter logistic curve (Liao and Liu, 2009):

y = D + \frac{A-D}{1 + \left( \frac{x}{C} \right)^B}
  • A = minimum effect
  • B = Hill slope
  • C = log D50 (an estimate that causes a 50% increase or decrease in response on the log scale)
  • D = maximum effect

example

A researcher is interested in testing the effect of a drug on a subject. 8 subjects are divided into 4 groups and each group is given a different dosage of the drug. The latency of a physical response is measured from each subject;

% Latency
Dose 1 Subject 1 2%
Subject 2 3%
Dose 2 Subject 3 5%
Subject 4 8%
Dose 3 Subject 5 4%
Subject 6 8%
Dose 4 Subject 7 7%
Subject 8 10%

statistical tests

Statistical tests that are commonly appropriate for this design:

  • Linear Regression
  • Generalized linear model
